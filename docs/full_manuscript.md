# Chance-constrained design of decentralized plastic-pyrolysis networks under feedstock-composition uncertainty: an EPR-compliance study for the Philippines

## Abstract

The Philippine Extended Producer Responsibility Act (RA 11898) obliges large enterprises to recover a rising share of their plastic-packaging footprint, reaching 80% from 2028, while the residual waste stream is dominated by multilayer and film fractions whose composition has never been measured at the locality level. We couple a validated pyrolysis and fractionation process model with a calibrated Gaussian-process surrogate and a two-stage stochastic spatial superstructure in which the recovery obligation appears in its statute-faithful, footprint-based form. The yield model reproduces independent mixture experiments to within 4.6 percentage points, and the reduced flowsheet matches six rigorous simulations to within 2% under leave-one-out cross-validation. Under an honestly wide composition uncertainty set, a network that processes its entire contracted footprint meets the obligation in only 24 to 30% of scenarios, and no siting decision changes this; a single gas-energy-recovery module raises compliance to 80%. The value of the stochastic solution is zero to within solver tolerance, for reasons we identify, so design value is quantified instead as the cost of assuring 90% compliance: 18 million pesos per year under mild spatial heterogeneity, against a median of 413 million under strong heterogeneity, where the assurance mechanism itself switches to selective contracting. Deterministic designs over-promise compliance by 25 to 30 percentage points, and feedstock composition alone carries 62% of the variance in net present value. Compliance and economics point to the same missing dataset: resin-resolved waste characterization.

# 1. Introduction

The Philippines generates roughly 2.7 million tonnes of plastic waste each year, much of it through a retail economy built on single-use sachets. These multilayer laminates and films defeat mechanical recycling, and the informal sector, which recovers most of what has value, leaves them behind (World Bank, 2021). The Extended Producer Responsibility Act of 2022 (RA 11898) turned this from an environmental statistic into a binding design constraint. Obliged enterprises must now recover a rising fraction of their own plastic-packaging footprint, starting at 20% for 2023 and reaching 80% from 2028, with fines of 5 to 20 million pesos or twice the recovery cost of the shortfall, whichever is higher (DENR, 2023). Pyrolysis to liquid fuel is one of the few end-of-life routes compatible with the contaminated multilayer fraction. The design question it raises is spatial and uncertain at the same time: at what scale and in what configuration can a pyrolysis network satisfy a footprint-based recovery obligation, at acceptable cost, when the feedstock composition varies from one locality to the next and has never been measured at the resin level?

The recent literature is further along than a casual gap statement would suggest, and we position this work against it by name. Wang and Maravelias (2024) established that composition matters for plastic supply-chain design. Their deterministic mixed-integer models track composition through the network, and they claim, correctly, to be the first to do so. Crîstiu et al. (2024), in this journal, optimized the choice between incineration and pyrolysis for mixed plastic waste in Northern Italy; they note composition variability and then hold it fixed. The distributed municipal-waste siting lineage runs back at least to Santibañez-Aguilar et al. (2013). On the methods side, surrogate-assisted flowsheet optimization has been mature at the single-flowsheet scale for some time (Caballero and Grossmann, 2008; Cozad et al., 2014; Bhosekar and Ierapetritou, 2018), and optimization under uncertainty offers a well-stocked toolbox (Sahinidis, 2004; Grossmann et al., 2016; Li and Grossmann, 2021), including robust formulations for the case where no distribution deserves trust (Bertsimas and Sim, 2004).

Three gaps nonetheless remain open, and they are only closed jointly. First, every network model we are aware of treats composition as a known input, either fixed across localities or deterministically tracked; none treats it as an empirically characterized random quantity whose variance propagates through product yields into the design. Second, the siting question has never been posed together with a binding recovery obligation of the RA 11898 type, in which the denominator of the constraint is the contracted packaging footprint itself. Third, surrogate acceleration has not been used to carry feedstock uncertainty into network-scale design; existing applications speed up a single flowsheet. Our contribution is the coupling of the three: a composition-uncertainty-aware surrogate of a validated pyrolysis and fractionation flowsheet, embedded exactly in a two-stage stochastic spatial superstructure, solved under the statutory obligation in both risk-neutral and chance-constrained form.

Two features of this study are findings rather than choices, and we flag them at the outset. The value of the stochastic solution turns out to be essentially zero for this problem class, for economic reasons we can name, and this redirects the design question from expected cost to compliance risk; the cost-of-assurance curve becomes the decision-relevant output. And because no Philippine dataset resolves municipal plastics by resin and locality, the composition uncertainty set had to be constructed rather than estimated. We report the sensitivity of the design conclusions to its spatial-heterogeneity parameter as a result in its own right, and it turns out to be the sharpest argument the study makes for commissioning the missing measurements. The motivating number deserves to be stated plainly: under an honestly wide uncertainty set, a network that processes its entire contracted footprint meets the 2028 obligation in 24% of composition scenarios.

Section 2 presents the process model and its two-stage validation, the uncertainty set, the surrogate, and the optimization formulations. Section 3 gives the results, Section 4 the discussion and limitations, and Section 5 the conclusions.

# 2. Methods

## 2.1 Process model architecture

No mainstream sequential-modular simulator provides a rigorous polymer-pyrolysis reactor, because the devolatilization of mixed solid plastic is a kinetically controlled process far from equilibrium, and no general-purpose unit operation exists for it. We therefore implemented the reactor as a yield-shift block. An external lumped-kinetic model computes product-lump yields as a function of feed composition and residence time; the flowsheet applies the resulting yield vector through a scripted unit operation and retains rigorous thermodynamics for everything downstream. We considered and rejected a Gibbs reactor: driving the product slate to equilibrium collapses it toward light gas and coke, destroying the oil and wax distribution on which fuel recovery depends. The yield-shift boundary also serves a second purpose. It is deliberately the seam at which the network-scale surrogate of Section 2.6 later replaces the process model, so the process and optimization layers share one validated interface.

## 2.2 Yield and conversion submodels

Product yields follow the linear superposition model of Genuino et al. (2023). The yield of lump $j \in \{\mathrm{GAS}, \mathrm{OIL/WAX}, \mathrm{SOLID}\}$ from a blend with mass fractions $x_i$ over feedstock categories $i$ is

$$Y_j(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i x_i\, Y_{ij}, \qquad (1)$$

where the $Y_{ij}$ are measured single-feedstock yields at 500 °C for five virgin resins and eight real-waste sorting categories of the German DKR-350 specification, the latter including the contaminated multilayer and film fractions that dominate Philippine residual streams. Genuino et al. report that superposition holds within 8 percentage points (pp) and document one specific interaction: above 33 wt% PET, oil and wax are over-predicted by up to 8 pp and solids under-predicted by up to 16 pp. We carry this correction as a piecewise term. It activates only above the threshold, which the Philippine composition set of Section 2.5 rarely reaches.

Devolatilization conversion and reactor sizing follow the first-order kinetics of Westerhout et al. (1997), with $X_i(T,\tau) = 1 - \exp[-k_i(T)\,\tau]$ per resin class, rate constants $k_i = k_{0,i}\exp(-E_{a,i}/RT)$ from their Table 4, and linear mixing across the blend. At the 500 °C design basis, 99% conversion of the slowest resin class requires 177 s of residence time, and this figure sizes the reactor in the economic model.

We verified the yield model against Genuino et al.'s own measured mixture data before using it. For the two virgin-polymer mixtures whose composition is exactly known (20 and 45 wt% PET), the worst absolute error across all lumps is 4.6 pp against a pre-registered acceptance criterion of 5 pp, and the model reproduces the documented direction of the PET interaction.

## 2.3 Rigorous flowsheet and its verification

The site-level flowsheet (Fig. 1b) was built in DWSIM with the Peng-Robinson equation of state. The choice is conventional for good reason: the fractionation feed is a non-polar hydrocarbon mixture spanning C1 to beyond C40, exactly the class of system for which cubic equations of state paired with petroleum pseudocomponents are the refinery standard, and activity-coefficient models offer nothing here while handling supercritical light gases worse. Three pseudocomponents represent the oil (naphtha, middle distillate, wax) and four light compounds represent the gas. The train comprises a quench, a gas-liquid flash, a stabilizer flash at 45 °C that keeps the naphtha condenser within cooling-water range in a tropical utility context, an atmospheric shortcut column for naphtha, and a vacuum column at 0.25 to 0.30 bar for the diesel and wax split. The vacuum holds the wax reboiler at 353 °C, inside the hot-oil utility envelope and safely below the onset of paraffin cracking near 370 °C.

The converged configuration of record closes its overall mass balance to 0.0014%. Product purities are 95.9 mol% for stabilized naphtha, 98.8% for the diesel cut, and 99.5% for wax after both column key specifications were tightened to 0.005. All verification actuals are tabulated in the repository, together with the two criteria that a mass-lump model cannot meaningfully satisfy: the elemental balance, and the reactor endotherm, which the energy audit carries instead of the flowsheet.

## 2.4 A reduced-order oracle, and what its validation taught us

Network-scale optimization needs thousands of flowsheet evaluations, which no desktop simulator can supply. We therefore built a reduced-order oracle that mirrors the flowsheet. It carries the verified yield and conversion submodels, a flash-coupling term in which naphtha loss to fuel gas scales with gas make, composition-dependent oil density, and the PET interaction, with duty and loss coefficients initially calibrated to the single converged base case.

Calibrating to one point is a limitation, and we tested it rather than merely stating it. Six additional DWSIM runs at compositions spanning the uncertainty set, from multilayer-heavy to polyolefin-rich to high-PET, produced 24 product-flow comparisons. We had registered a failure prediction in advance: extrapolation error concentrated at the highest-gas-make case. The data falsified it. The errors were composition-independent biases with identifiable mechanisms. Wax was low by 5.55% in all six cases, which traced to the vacuum column's heavy-key specification, since 2 mol% of leakage corresponds to about 5.3 mass% at the pseudocomponent molar-mass ratio. Naphtha was uniformly high by 2.5%, which traced to loss coefficients calibrated against a superseded column configuration. A two-parameter least-squares fit on the 24 cells, one wax-transfer fraction and one loss-scale multiplier, both mechanistically interpretable, reduced the worst error to 1.6%, with every cell within 2% and total liquid recovery, the quantity that enters the regulatory constraint, within 0.26%. Because fitting and testing on the same cells would invite a fair objection, we also ran leave-one-case-out cross-validation: the worst held-out error is 1.87% and the mean is 0.52%, so the correction generalizes rather than memorizes. It is worth recording that total liquid was already within 1% before any recalibration; the biases lived entirely in the cut split.

That cut split, the division of the oil into naphtha, middle distillate, and wax, is treated throughout as a declared uncertain parameter rather than a point value, because the underlying experiments characterize the oil by carbon type (¹³C NMR) and not by boiling distribution. A Dirichlet distribution centred on the flowsheet realization (0.356/0.406/0.238, with a standard deviation near ±0.07 per cut, consistent with literature ranges for polyolefin pyrolysis oil at 500 °C) is propagated through all economic results. The choice has a provable separation property. Because Eq. (1) is linear and the split acts downstream of the total-liquid quantity, the variance the split contributes to liquid recovery, and hence to the regulatory constraint and to every siting decision variable, is exactly zero. The split moves only product valuation, which we report as a bounded band.

## 2.5 The feedstock-composition uncertainty set

No published Philippine dataset resolves municipal plastic waste at the resin level by locality. Waste analysis and characterization studies report plastics as a single fraction, and the national protocol does not require finer resolution. The uncertainty set is therefore constructed, not estimated, and we treat that scarcity as part of the problem statement rather than an inconvenience. The set lives on the 8-simplex of the DKR-350 categories for which verified yields exist. Its central vector encodes what is qualitatively certain about the Philippine residual stream, namely the dominance of films and multilayer sachet laminates and the depletion of PET by informal-sector recovery, with magnitudes flagged as engineering estimates. A Dirichlet distribution with concentration $c = 25$ represents that ignorance honestly; the PE-film fraction, for instance, carries a 95% band from 0.12 to 0.46. A boxed support (central ±2σ, clipped) is exported in parallel, so the whole analysis can be re-posed in robust or distributionally robust form if the distributional assumption is ever challenged. Spatial heterogeneity between local-government-unit (LGU) catchments is modelled hierarchically, as a national draw followed by LGU-level Dirichlet draws with concentration $c_{\mathrm{LGU}}$. No data constrain $c_{\mathrm{LGU}}$, so we report results for a mild regime (60) and a strong one (15) and treat the sensitivity of the design conclusions to this parameter as a finding.

## 2.6 Surrogate model and exact embedding

A Gaussian-process (GP) surrogate with an anisotropic RBF kernel and a white-noise term was trained on an 800-point design, 200 space-filling compositions on the box-filtered simplex crossed with four residence times, evaluated through the validated oracle. A gradient-boosting benchmark (LightGBM) ran alongside it. The GP matched or beat the benchmark on every output, and unlike the benchmark it carries a predictive variance whose calibration we tested rather than assumed: empirical 95%-interval coverage on held-out data is 0.99 to 1.00 across outputs.

Embedding the surrogate in the optimization exploits a structural property instead of fighting it. Because Eq. (1) is linear in composition and the fitted flowsheet corrections are linear in the relevant flows, the response of a site to a blended feed equals the flow-weighted sum of the per-source responses. Coefficients evaluated once per source and scenario are therefore exact within the linear program; no blending approximation enters the network model at all. The surrogate contributes two things: scenario-coefficient generation at negligible cost, and a calibrated predictive variance available to risk-aware formulations. With a higher-fidelity oracle the same architecture applies unchanged, with the exactness property replaced by a stated mixing approximation.

## 2.7 Two-stage stochastic superstructure and chance-constrained design

The spatial instance covers eight Metro Manila LGU catchments, amounting to 130.2 kt per year of network-available plastic at a capture fraction of 0.5, and four candidate sites with discrete capacity levels. Stage-one binaries select site openings and sizes $z_{sk}$, gas-energy-recovery modules $g_s$, and LGU contracting decisions $c_i$; stage-two recourse variables are the source-to-site flows $x_{isw}$ in each scenario $w$. With scenario probability $p_w$, per-source recovery and revenue coefficients $r_{iw}$ and $v_{iw}$ from the surrogate, gas coefficients $\gamma_{iw}$, transport and operating costs $t_{is}$ and $o$, and annualized capital $K$, the risk-neutral program reads

$$\min\; \sum_{s,k} K_{sk} z_{sk} + \sum_s K^g g_s \;+\; \sum_w p_w \Big[ \sum_{i,s} (t_{is} + o - v_{iw})\,x_{isw} + \pi_w \Big] \qquad (2)$$

subject to capacity, availability ($\sum_s x_{isw} \le W_{iw} c_i$), and the recovery obligation of RA 11898 in footprint form,

$$\sigma_w \;\ge\; \rho \sum_i W_{iw} c_i \;-\; \sum_{i,s} r_{iw} x_{isw} \;-\; \sum_s \mathrm{cred}_{sw}, \qquad (3)$$

where $\rho = 0.80$ is the 2028 obligation, the credited term $\mathrm{cred}_{sw} \le \eta \sum_i \gamma_{iw} x_{isw}$ is available only at sites with a gas module, and the penalty $\pi_w = \max(\pi^{\mathrm{unit}}\sigma_w,\; F\, b_w)$ reproduces the statute's dual fine structure, twice the recovery cost of the shortfall or the tiered lump fine, whichever binds, with $b_w$ a scenario-noncompliance binary. Four modelling assumptions are stated explicitly. A1: the obligation denominator is the full contracted footprint, so a network cannot comply by cherry-picking within a contract. A2: the capture fraction is 0.5. A3: the hierarchical composition scenarios of Section 2.5. A4: $\rho = 0.80$ at steady state.

Beyond the risk-neutral program, a chance-constrained variant imposes $\Pr[\text{recovery} \ge \rho] \ge 1-\varepsilon$ by scenario counting, $\sum_w b_w \le \varepsilon S$, and the difference between its optimum and the risk-neutral optimum defines the cost of compliance assurance, the central design quantity of the study. Enforcing at most ten violations among $S = 100$ scenarios is a sample statement, not a population one; the corresponding one-sided 95% Clopper-Pearson lower bound on the true compliance probability is 0.836, and we phrase the guarantee accordingly. All programs are mixed-integer linear and were solved with HiGHS, at a relative gap of 10⁻⁴ wherever value-of-stochastic-solution accounting was performed, because a conventional gap of 5×10⁻³ demonstrably manufactures small negative VSS values inside solver tolerance, and at 10⁻³ within the sampling study. Sample-average-approximation stability was assessed with ten independent replications of one hundred scenarios per heterogeneity regime, reporting confidence intervals on assurance costs and the replication frequency of each design feature, which is what separates structural decisions from sampling noise. The deterministic benchmark solves the identical model on the mean composition and is then evaluated under the full scenario set, which yields both the value of the stochastic solution and the deterministic design's compliance over-promise.


**Nomenclature (Section 2.7).** $z_{sk}$: site s opened at size k (binary). $g_s$: gas-energy-recovery module at site s (binary). $c_i$: catchment i contracted (binary). $x_{isw}$: flow from catchment i to site s in scenario w (t yr⁻¹). $W_{iw}$: available waste of catchment i in scenario w. $r_{iw}, v_{iw}, \gamma_{iw}$: liquid-recovery, revenue, and gas coefficients per tonne. $\sigma_w$: recovery shortfall. $\pi_w$: penalty. $b_w$: noncompliance indicator. $\rho$: obligation fraction (0.80). $\eta$: gas-credit efficiency (0.9). $\varepsilon$: allowed violation fraction. $K_{sk}, K^g$: annualized capital.

# 3. Results

## 3.1 Compliance risk is chemistry-bound

Figure 2 shows the distribution of liquid recovery over 200 compositions drawn from the uncertainty set, at both fidelity levels of the process model. At the reactor level, the probability of meeting the 80% obligation is 38%. Passing the same compositions through the full validated flowsheet, where flash and stabilizer losses and unconverted material all count against the recovered liquid, lowers it to 24%. Each added layer of fidelity makes compliance harder, not easier, so the reactor-level number is an upper bound on anything liquid-only accounting can deliver.

At the network level this risk turns out to be irreducible by siting. Product revenues, around 36,200 pesos per tonne at central prices, dominate every cost term, so all optimal designs process the entire contracted footprint. Recovery then equals the mass-weighted mean yield of the stream, and no arrangement of plants can change that quantity. Every all-processing configuration achieves a 30% compliance probability over the fifty-scenario set, identically for the stochastic and the deterministic design, because the constraint binds on chemistry rather than logistics. The one decision that moves the number is not spatial at all. A single gas-energy-recovery module at the central site, costing 15 million pesos per year annualized and crediting fuel-gas energy recovery toward the obligation at η = 0.9, lifts compliance from 30% to 80%. In the replication study of Section 3.3 this module is selected at the central site in ten of ten mild-heterogeneity replications and eight of ten strong ones. It is the single structurally robust design feature of the study.

## 3.2 The value of the stochastic solution is zero, and that is the finding

Across the base case and the full four-lever model, at both heterogeneity regimes, the value of the stochastic solution (VSS) is zero to within 0.012% of the objective at a relative MIP gap of 10⁻⁴. At the coarser gap of 5×10⁻³ the same experiments return small negative VSS values, which are theoretically impossible and serve as a useful warning: VSS accounting at loose solver tolerances manufactures artifacts. Two mechanisms explain the genuine zero. First, the statutory penalty scale, 20 million pesos in lump fines or twice the recovery cost of the shortfall, sits two orders of magnitude below network operating profit of roughly 2.7 billion pesos per year at upper-bound economics, so a risk-neutral expectation barely registers non-compliance. Second, the one hedge that matters, the gas module, is already optimal at the mean composition, because mean liquid recovery of 77.0% itself falls short of the obligation; Jensen-type under-hedging never gets a chance to operate. The conclusion generalizes beyond this instance. For profit-dominant valorization networks facing compliance obligations whose fines are small relative to margins, VSS systematically understates the value of stochastic modelling, because the value resides in the risk constraint rather than the expected cost. The chance-constrained formulation is the appropriate lens, and the rest of the results are organized around it.

## 3.3 The cost of compliance assurance

Table 3 and Fig. 5 report the study's central design quantity: the minimum additional annual cost of requiring compliance in at least ninety of one hundred scenarios (a sample guarantee whose 95% lower confidence bound on the true probability is 0.836), relative to the risk-neutral optimum, from ten independent sample-average-approximation replications per heterogeneity regime.

Under mild spatial heterogeneity ($c_{\mathrm{LGU}} = 60$) assurance is cheap and mechanistically uniform: 18.3 ± 9.9 million pesos per year (mean ± 95% CI; median 10.0; range 3.6 to 41.5), achieved in every replication by hardware alone, a second gas module plus at most one capacity upsize. No replication drops a single LGU. Under strong heterogeneity ($c_{\mathrm{LGU}} = 15$) the mean rises to 849 ± 635 million, but the mean is the wrong summary here, because the distribution is trimodal and the assurance mechanism itself switches between modes. Four of ten replications stay in the hardware regime, at 6.5 to 22.5 million. Four move to selective contracting, declining to serve one high-risk catchment, at 209 to 1,414 million, a cost dominated by foregone revenue. Two find that the only way to guarantee compliance is to contract no footprint at all, the exit solutions discussed in Section 3.5. The median of 413 million per year is the honest central figure, roughly twenty times the mild-regime cost. Which LGU gets dropped, when one does, is pure sampling noise; across replications it is Quezon City four times, Caloocan and Manila three times each, and others at most twice, so no catchment is structurally condemned. What is structural is that selective contracting activates at all once spatial heterogeneity is strong. Since no Philippine dataset currently constrains within-metro resin heterogeneity, the twenty-fold spread between the regimes is itself the study's sharpest argument for commissioning resin-resolved waste characterization.

## 3.4 Deterministic designs over-promise compliance by 25 to 30 percentage points

The deterministic benchmark, the same model solved at the mean composition and then evaluated under the full scenario set, believes itself fully compliant. At the mean, with its gas module, the shortfall is zero and the design promises 100% compliance. Realized compliance under uncertainty is 75% in the mild regime and 70% in the strong one, an over-promise of 25 to 30 pp, or one violation year in every three to four. Composition-blind fixed-yield formulations, the standard in the deterministic waste-network literature, cannot even represent this risk, since a single yield coefficient admits no distribution at all. This quantifies, for the constraint that motivates the network's existence, exactly what the deterministic assumption conceals, independently of whether any money is lost in expectation. Section 3.2 shows essentially none is.

## 3.5 An artifact our own replication protocol exposed: vacuous compliance by exit

Two strong-regime replications satisfied the chance constraint by contracting zero footprint. With no obligation denominator, the constraint holds vacuously, and guaranteed compliance is achieved by declining to participate. The formulation is faithful to the statute's letter, since producer-responsibility organizations do contract voluntarily, but the solution forfeits the entire operating profit of roughly 2.6 billion pesos per year and is regulatorily absurd. We report it rather than suppress it, for two reasons. The replication protocol found it, which is what validation protocols are for. And it identifies a concrete refinement, a minimum-service constraint or a separate no-build branch, that any statute-faithful compliance formulation of this class will need.

## 3.6 Design and economic synthesis

Every solve in this study, under every regime and formulation, selects the same spatial skeleton: one small northern satellite of 15 to 30 kt per year plus one large central plant of 120 kt per year. Full centralization never wins, and neither does full decentralization. Scale economies, with a capacity exponent of 0.65, dominate transport costs of roughly 240 pesos per tonne, yet the satellite persists for the northern catchments. The economics, propagated over the full uncertainty set with ten thousand Monte Carlo draws across composition, prices, and ±30% on capital and operating cost, are strong but deliberately reported in upper-bound form. The break-even blended fuel price is 15.1 pesos per liter (P5 to P95: 11.7 to 18.7) against a verified ex-plant diesel parity of 51 to 55. We prefer to state this as cost headroom: the design absorbs 26,000 to 30,000 pesos per tonne of costs the model omits, feedstock acquisition, collection, sorting, oil upgrading, land, and working capital, before net present value reaches zero, which is roughly twice literature-class estimates for those items combined. Global sensitivity analysis (Fig. 6) closes the argument. Feedstock oil yield, which is to say composition, carries a total Sobol index of 0.62 on NPV, more than all product prices and the capital range combined. The same uncertainty that breaks the compliance guarantee also drives the economics, and both point at the same missing dataset.

## 3.7 Robustness of the conclusions to the constructed set and to prices

Two checks probe the two assumptions a skeptical reader will probe first. The national Dirichlet concentration, fixed at c = 25 in the main analysis, was varied across {10, 25, 50} with two thousand draws each. Mean recovery barely moves (77.3 to 77.6%), because the mean is set by the central composition, but the compliance probability runs 36%, 30%, and 19% respectively. The direction is instructive: tightening knowledge of the composition makes non-compliance more certain, not less, because the mean sits below the target at every concentration and a narrower set simply removes the favourable upper tail. Better information cannot rescue a sub-threshold mean; only the accounting rule or the feedstock can. At the widest set the network conclusions hold as well: the gas module at the central site is still selected, the hybrid layout persists, and risk-neutral compliance is 78%. The cost of assurance, however, grows with total composition uncertainty in whichever layer carries it; at c = 10 the chance-constrained solution resorts to selective contracting even in the mild spatial regime.

Prices were pushed jointly to their lower bounds, a reduction of roughly 22% on every product. The optimal design is unchanged: the same sites, the same gas module, all eight catchments contracted. Prices scale the objective without moving its argmax in this instance, which is consistent with the global sensitivity analysis, where all price factors together carry less than a quarter of the variance that composition does. Stage-two revenue uncertainty is therefore second-order for the design, though not for the valuation, where it is carried explicitly in the Monte Carlo bands.

# 4. Discussion

**Relation to prior work.** Wang and Maravelias (2024) showed that composition-awareness changes plastic supply-chain design under a deterministic model. Our results show what the deterministic assumption hides, a 25 to 30 pp over-promise on the very constraint that motivates the network, while confirming their structural point, since composition dominates our economics too, with a total Sobol index of 0.62 on NPV. Relative to the deterministic siting lineage (Santibañez-Aguilar et al., 2013; Crîstiu et al., 2024), the spatial answer itself barely moves: a hybrid configuration, never pure centralization or pure decentralization, robust across every regime and formulation we solved. Uncertainty does not change where to build. It changes what can be guaranteed, and at what price.

**When VSS misleads.** We argue the near-zero value of the stochastic solution is a property of a recognizable problem class, not a peculiarity of this instance: valorization networks whose product margins dwarf their compliance penalties, and whose dominant hedge is already visible at the mean. In that class, the standard two-stage expected-cost comparison will report that uncertainty does not matter while a quarter of scenarios violate the binding regulation. What a decision-maker actually needs is the cost-of-assurance curve, the price of a chance-constrained guarantee as a function of the guarantee level and the uncertainty's structure, and that quantity is invisible to VSS accounting. One numerical hazard deserves emphasis because it has practical teeth: at a conventional 0.5% MIP gap, our VSS estimates came out spuriously negative. The accounting is only meaningful at tolerances an order of magnitude tighter than the quantity being measured.

**Policy reading.** Three implications follow directly from the numbers, and we state the first conditionally because it turns on regulatory interpretation. If fuel-gas energy recovery is creditable toward the obligation under the implementing rules' diversion definitions, then one 15-million-peso-per-year module moves compliance from 30% to 80%, making that accounting question the single most consequential rule in the system; if it is not creditable, the 80% obligation is close to infeasible for realistic Philippine residual streams regardless of network design, because compliance is chemistry-bound. Second, statutory fines at their current scale do not economically force compliance for networks of this profitability; the binding arm of the penalty is the twice-recovery-cost provision, and enforcement design should recognize which arm actually binds. Third, the twenty-fold spread in assurance cost between heterogeneity regimes, a parameter no existing dataset constrains, makes resin-resolved, locality-level waste characterization the cheapest risk-reduction instrument available. Both the compliance analysis and the economic sensitivity point at that same measurement.

**Limitations.** The composition uncertainty set is constructed from verified qualitative structure and flagged magnitudes, not estimated from resin-resolved data; that is the premise of the study, and the exported robust set allows the whole analysis to be re-posed distribution-free if the premise is challenged. The economics are deliberately upper-bound, with no feedstock acquisition, collection, sorting, upgrading, land, or working capital, and are reported as cost headroom; if realized costs consume the 26,000 to 30,000 pesos per tonne of headroom, penalties become material and the VSS conclusion would need revisiting. The yield basis is a single temperature, 500 °C, matching the underlying experiments; a temperature-resolved yield surface is a natural extension. The oracle is validated at six rigorous-simulation points spanning the set, with leave-one-out confirmation, rather than densely across it. The spatial instance is a prototype of eight catchments and four sites with population-derived generation. The formulation is static, addressing the 2028 steady-state obligation of 80% rather than the 2023-to-2028 ramp. We argue the steady state is the binding case: a design that meets 80% in only a minority of scenarios passes the 20 to 50% obligations of the early years with room to spare, so the ramp adds capacity-timing refinement, not feasibility risk, and a multi-period extension is deferred on those grounds. The chance-constrained variant also needs a minimum-service refinement to exclude the vacuous exit solution that our own replication protocol exposed.

# 5. Conclusions

For a Metro Manila mixed-plastic pyrolysis network under the RA 11898 recovery obligation, four conclusions hold. First, compliance risk is chemistry-bound: a network processing its full contracted footprint meets the 80% obligation in 24 to 30% of composition scenarios, no siting decision changes this, and one gas-energy-recovery module at 15 million pesos per year raises compliance to 80%. Second, the value of the stochastic solution is zero to within solver tolerance, for identifiable economic reasons, and the decision-relevant quantity is instead the cost of compliance assurance: 18 million pesos per year under mild spatial heterogeneity against a median of 413 million under strong heterogeneity, where the mechanism switches from hardware hedging to selective contracting. Third, deterministic designs over-promise compliance by 25 to 30 percentage points. Fourth, feedstock composition carries 62% of the variance in net present value, more than all prices and capital uncertainty combined. The spatial answer is robustly hybrid. The highest-value action outside the model is a measurement: resin-resolved, locality-level waste characterization, for which this study supplies two independent quantitative justifications.
