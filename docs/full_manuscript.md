# Chance-constrained design of decentralized plastic-pyrolysis networks under feedstock-composition uncertainty: an EPR-compliance study for the Philippines

## Abstract

The Philippine Extended Producer Responsibility Act (RA 11898) obliges large enterprises to recover a rising share of their plastic-packaging footprint — 80% from 2028 — while the residual waste stream is dominated by multilayer and film fractions whose composition is unmeasured at the locality level. We couple a validated pyrolysis–fractionation process model (yield superposition verified to 4.6 pp; six-point rigorous-simulation validation to ≤2%) with a calibrated Gaussian-process surrogate and a two-stage stochastic spatial superstructure in which the recovery obligation is embedded in statute-faithful footprint form. Under an honestly wide composition uncertainty set, a network that processes its entire contracted footprint meets the obligation in only 24–30% of scenarios, and no siting decision changes this; a single gas-energy-recovery module raises compliance to 80%. The value of the stochastic solution is provably ≈ 0 — fines are two orders of magnitude below margins — so design value is quantified instead as the cost of 90% compliance assurance: ₱18 M yr⁻¹ under mild spatial heterogeneity versus a median ₱413 M yr⁻¹, with a mechanism switch to selective contracting, under strong heterogeneity. Deterministic designs over-promise compliance by 25–30 percentage points, and feedstock composition carries 62% of the variance of net present value. Both compliance and economics point to the same missing dataset: resin-resolved waste characterization.

# 1. Introduction

The Philippines generates on the order of 2.7 million tonnes of plastic waste annually, within a retail economy organized around single-use sachets — multilayer laminates and films that mechanical recycling cannot process and that informal-sector recovery leaves behind (World Bank, 2021). The Extended Producer Responsibility Act of 2022 (RA 11898) converts this from an environmental statistic into a binding design constraint: obliged enterprises must recover a rising fraction of their own plastic-packaging footprint — 20% for 2023 rising to 80% from 2028 — with fines of ₱5–20 million or twice the recovery cost of the shortfall, whichever is higher (DENR, 2023). Pyrolysis to liquid fuel is one of the few end-of-life routes compatible with the contaminated multilayer fraction, and the design question it raises is inherently spatial and inherently uncertain: at what scale and configuration can a pyrolysis network satisfy a footprint-based recovery obligation, at acceptable cost, given feedstock whose composition varies across localities and is, at the resin level, unmeasured?

Recent literature is more advanced than a naive gap statement would suggest, and we position against it by name. Wang and Maravelias (2024) established that composition matters for plastic-waste supply-chain design, presenting deterministic mixed-integer models that track composition through the network and claiming — correctly — first-mover status on composition-aware design. Crîstiu et al. (2024), in this journal, optimized incineration-versus-pyrolysis siting for mixed plastic waste in Northern Italy, noting composition variability and then treating it as fixed scenarios. The distributed municipal-waste siting lineage runs back through Santibañez-Aguilar et al. (2013). On the methods side, surrogate-assisted flowsheet optimization is mature at single-flowsheet scale (Caballero and Grossmann, 2008; Cozad et al., 2014; Bhosekar and Ierapetritou, 2018), as is optimization under uncertainty as a toolset (Sahinidis, 2004; Grossmann et al., 2016; Li and Grossmann, 2021), with robust counterparts available where distributions are untrustworthy (Bertsimas and Sim, 2004).

Three gaps nonetheless remain jointly unaddressed. First, prior network models treat composition as a known input — fixed across localities, or at most deterministically tracked — never as an empirically characterized stochastic quantity whose variance propagates through product yields into the design. Second, the siting question has never been posed jointly with a binding recovery obligation of the RA 11898 type, in which the constraint's denominator is the contracted packaging footprint. Third, surrogate acceleration has not been used to carry feedstock-composition uncertainty into network-scale design; existing applications accelerate a single flowsheet. The contribution of this paper is the coupling of the three: a composition-uncertainty-aware surrogate of a validated pyrolysis–fractionation flowsheet, embedded exactly in a two-stage stochastic spatial superstructure, solved under the statutory recovery obligation in both risk-neutral and chance-constrained form.

Two features of the study are findings in their own right rather than methodological choices. The value of the stochastic solution turns out to be provably near zero for this problem class, for identifiable economic reasons, which redirects the design question from expected cost to compliance risk — and motivates the cost-of-assurance curve as the decision-relevant output. And because no Philippine dataset resolves municipal plastics at the resin level by locality, the composition uncertainty set is constructed rather than estimated; the sensitivity of design conclusions to its spatial-heterogeneity parameter is reported as a result, and turns out to be the study's sharpest argument for commissioning the missing measurements. The motivating number is stated up front: under the honestly wide uncertainty set, a network processing its entire contracted footprint meets the 2028 obligation in 24% of composition scenarios.

Section 2 presents the process model, its two-stage validation, the uncertainty set, the surrogate, and the optimization formulations; Section 3 the results; Section 4 discusses implications, limitations, and the policy reading; Section 5 concludes.

# 2. Methods

## 2.1 Process model architecture

Neither DWSIM nor any mainstream sequential-modular simulator provides a rigorous polymer-pyrolysis reactor, because devolatilization of mixed solid plastic is a kinetically controlled, far-from-equilibrium process for which no general built-in unit operation exists. The reactor was therefore implemented as a *yield-shift* block: an external lumped-kinetic model computes product-lump yields as a function of feed composition and residence time, and the flowsheet applies the resulting yield vector through a scripted unit operation, retaining rigorous thermodynamics for all downstream separation. A Gibbs (equilibrium) reactor was explicitly rejected: driving the product slate to thermodynamic equilibrium collapses it toward light gas and coke and destroys the oil/wax distribution on which fuel recovery depends. The yield-shift boundary is also deliberately the seam at which the network-scale surrogate (Section 2.6) later replaces the process model, so that the process and optimization layers share a single, validated interface.

## 2.2 Yield and conversion submodels

Product yields follow the linear superposition model of Genuino et al. (2023), in which the yield of lump $j \in \{\mathrm{GAS}, \mathrm{OIL/WAX}, \mathrm{SOLID}\}$ from a blend with mass fractions $x_i$ over feedstock categories $i$ is

$$Y_j(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i x_i\, Y_{ij}, \qquad (1)$$

where $Y_{ij}$ are measured single-feedstock yields at 500 °C for five virgin resins and eight DKR-350 real-waste sorting categories, the latter including the contaminated multilayer and film fractions that dominate Philippine residual streams. Genuino et al. report that superposition holds within 8 percentage points (pp) and document a specific interaction above 33 wt% PET, in which oil/wax is over-predicted (up to 8 pp) and solids under-predicted (up to 16 pp); this correction is carried as a piecewise term and activates only above the threshold, which the Philippine composition set of Section 2.5 rarely reaches.

Devolatilization conversion and reactor sizing follow the first-order kinetics of Westerhout et al. (1997), with $X_i(T,\tau) = 1 - \exp[-k_i(T)\,\tau]$ per resin class, $k_i = k_{0,i}\exp(-E_{a,i}/RT)$ from their Table 4, and linear mixing across the blend. At the 500 °C design basis the residence time for 99% conversion of the slowest resin class is 177 s, which sets reactor volume in the economic model.

The yield model was verified against Genuino et al.'s own measured mixture data before use. For the two virgin-polymer mixtures with exactly known composition (20 and 45 wt% PET), the worst absolute error across all lumps is 4.6 pp against a pre-registered 5 pp acceptance criterion, and the model reproduces the documented direction of the PET interaction (`verify_vs_genuino.py`).

## 2.3 Rigorous flowsheet and verification

The site-level flowsheet (Fig. 1b) was built in DWSIM with the Peng–Robinson equation of state, selected because the fractionation feed is a non-polar hydrocarbon mixture spanning C1–C40+ over a wide boiling range, the class of system for which cubic equations of state paired with petroleum pseudocomponents are the refinery standard; activity-coefficient models offer no advantage here and are weaker for supercritical light gases. The oil is represented by three pseudocomponents (naphtha, middle distillate, wax) and the gas by four light compounds. The train comprises quench, gas–liquid flash, a stabilizer flash (45 °C) that keeps the naphtha condenser within cooling-water range in a tropical utility context, an atmospheric shortcut column for naphtha, and a vacuum column (0.25/0.30 bar) for the diesel/wax split, which holds the wax reboiler at 353 °C — inside the hot-oil utility envelope and below the ~370 °C onset of thermal cracking of paraffin wax.

The converged configuration of record closes the overall mass balance to 0.0014%, with product purities of 95.9 mol% (stabilized naphtha), 98.8% (diesel cut), and 99.5% (wax) after both column key specifications were tightened to 0.005. All verification actuals, together with the two criteria that are inapplicable by construction to a mass-lump model (elemental balance; reactor endotherm, which is carried in the energy audit rather than the flowsheet), are tabulated in the repository (`flowsheet_spec.md`).

## 2.4 Reduced-order oracle and multi-point validation

Network-scale optimization requires thousands of flowsheet evaluations, which a desktop simulator cannot supply. A reduced-order oracle mirrors the flowsheet: it carries the verified yield and conversion submodels, a flash-coupling term in which naphtha loss to fuel gas scales with gas make, composition-dependent oil density, and the PET interaction, with duty and loss coefficients initially calibrated to the single converged base case.

Single-point calibration is a stated limitation, and it was tested: six additional DWSIM runs at compositions spanning the uncertainty set (high multilayer, high polyolefin, high PET, high polystyrene, high contamination) produced 24 product-flow comparisons. The pre-registered failure prediction — extrapolation error concentrated at the high-gas-make case — was falsified. Instead the errors were composition-independent biases with identifiable mechanisms: a −5.55% offset on wax in all six cases, traced to the vacuum column's heavy-key specification (2 mol% ≈ 5.3 mass% at the pseudocomponent molar-mass ratio), and a uniform +2.5% on naphtha, traced to loss coefficients calibrated against a superseded column configuration. A two-parameter least-squares fit on the 24 cells (a wax-transfer fraction and a loss-scale multiplier, both mechanistically interpretable) reduced the worst error to 1.6%, with all 24 cells within 2% and total liquid recovery — the quantity entering the regulatory constraint — within 0.26% (`verify_vs_dwsim.py`). Total liquid was within 1% even before recalibration; the biases resided entirely in the cut split.

The naphtha/middle/wax split of the oil is treated as a declared uncertain parameter rather than a point value, because the underlying experimental characterization is by carbon type (¹³C NMR) rather than boiling distribution. A Dirichlet distribution centred on the flowsheet realization (0.356/0.406/0.238, σ ≈ ±0.07 per cut, consistent with literature ranges for polyolefin pyrolysis oil at 500 °C) is propagated through all economic results. This choice has a provable separation property: because Eq. (1) is linear and the split acts downstream of the total-liquid quantity, the variance contributed by the split to liquid recovery — and hence to the regulatory constraint and every siting decision variable — is exactly zero; the split affects only product valuation, which is reported as a bounded band (`cut_split_uncertainty.py`).

## 2.5 Feedstock-composition uncertainty set

No published Philippine dataset resolves municipal plastic waste at the resin level by locality: waste analysis and characterization studies (WACS) report plastics as a single fraction, and the national characterization protocol does not mandate resin resolution. The composition uncertainty set is therefore constructed, not estimated, and this data scarcity is itself part of the problem statement. The set lives on the 8-simplex of the DKR-350 categories for which verified yields exist. Its central vector encodes the qualitatively verified structure of the Philippine residual stream — dominance of films and multilayer sachet laminates, and depletion of PET by informal-sector recovery — with magnitudes flagged as engineering estimates. A Dirichlet distribution with concentration $c = 25$ represents that ignorance honestly (e.g., the PE-film fraction carries a 95% band of 0.12–0.46), and a boxed support (central ±2σ, clipped) is exported in parallel so that the entire optimization can be re-posed in a robust or distributionally robust form if the distributional assumption is challenged. Spatial heterogeneity between LGU catchments is modelled hierarchically — a national draw followed by LGU-level Dirichlet draws with concentration $c_{\mathrm{LGU}}$ — and, because no data constrain $c_{\mathrm{LGU}}$, results are reported for a mild (60) and a strong (15) regime; the sensitivity of design conclusions to this parameter is treated as a finding, not a nuisance.

## 2.6 Surrogate model and exact embedding

A Gaussian-process (GP) surrogate with anisotropic RBF kernel and white-noise term was trained on an 800-point design (200 space-filling compositions on the box-filtered simplex × 4 residence times) evaluated through the validated oracle, against a gradient-boosting benchmark (LightGBM). The GP matched or exceeded the benchmark on every output and, unlike it, carries a predictive variance whose calibration was tested rather than assumed: empirical 95%-interval coverage on held-out data is 0.99–1.00 across outputs (`surrogate_train.py`).

Embedding in the optimization exploits a structural property rather than fighting it. Because Eq. (1) is linear in composition and the fitted flowsheet corrections are linear in the relevant flows, the site-level response to a blended feed equals the flow-weighted sum of per-source responses. Per-(source, scenario) coefficients evaluated once by the surrogate are therefore *exact* within the linear program — no blending approximation enters the network model. The surrogate's roles are scenario-coefficient generation at negligible cost and the calibrated predictive variance available to risk-aware formulations; at higher-fidelity oracles the same architecture applies unchanged, with the linearity property replaced by a stated mixing approximation.

## 2.7 Two-stage stochastic superstructure and chance-constrained design

The spatial instance covers eight Metro Manila LGU catchments (130.2 kt yr⁻¹ of network-available plastic at a 0.5 capture fraction) and four candidate sites with discrete capacity levels. Stage-1 (here-and-now) binaries select site openings and sizes $z_{sk}$, gas-energy-recovery modules $g_s$, and LGU contracting decisions $c_i$; stage-2 recourse variables are source-to-site flows $x_{isw}$ per scenario $w$. With scenario probability $p_w$, per-source recovery and revenue coefficients $r_{iw}$, $v_{iw}$ from the surrogate, gas coefficients $\gamma_{iw}$, transport and operating costs $t_{is}$, $o$, and annualized capital $K$:

$$\min\; \sum_{s,k} K_{sk} z_{sk} + \sum_s K^g g_s \;+\; \sum_w p_w \Big[ \sum_{i,s} (t_{is} + o - v_{iw})\,x_{isw} + \pi_w \Big] \qquad (2)$$

subject to capacity, availability ($\sum_s x_{isw} \le W_{iw} c_i$), and the recovery obligation of RA 11898 in footprint form:

$$\sigma_w \;\ge\; \rho \sum_i W_{iw} c_i \;-\; \sum_{i,s} r_{iw} x_{isw} \;-\; \sum_s \mathrm{cred}_{sw}, \qquad (3)$$

where $\rho = 0.80$ is the 2028 obligation, the credited term $\mathrm{cred}_{sw} \le \eta \sum_i \gamma_{iw} x_{isw}$ is available only where a gas module is built, and the penalty $\pi_w = \max(\pi^{\mathrm{unit}}\sigma_w,\; F\cdot b_w)$ reproduces the statute's dual fine structure (twice the cost of recovery of the shortfall, or the tiered lump fine, whichever binds), with $b_w$ a scenario-noncompliance binary. Four modelling assumptions are stated explicitly: (A1) the obligation denominator is the full contracted footprint, so the network cannot comply by cherry-picking within a contract; (A2) capture fraction 0.5; (A3) the hierarchical composition scenarios of Section 2.5; (A4) $\rho = 0.80$ steady state.

Beyond the risk-neutral program, a chance-constrained variant imposes $\Pr[\text{recovery} \ge \rho] \ge 1-\varepsilon$ by scenario counting ($\sum_w b_w \le \varepsilon S$), and the difference between its optimum and the risk-neutral optimum defines the *cost of compliance assurance* — the study's central design quantity. All programs are mixed-integer linear, solved with HiGHS to a 10⁻⁴ relative gap for value-of-stochastic-solution accounting (a coarser gap of 5×10⁻³ was shown to manufacture spurious negative VSS within solver tolerance) and 10⁻³ within the sampling study. Sample-average-approximation stability was assessed with ten independent replications of 100 scenarios per heterogeneity regime, reporting confidence intervals on assurance costs and the replication frequency of each design feature, which separates structural decisions from sampling noise. The deterministic benchmark solves the same model on the mean composition and is then evaluated under the full scenario set (expected result of the expected-value solution), yielding both the value of the stochastic solution and the deterministic design's compliance over-promise.


# 3. Results

## 3.1 Compliance risk is chemistry-bound

Figure 2 shows the distribution of liquid recovery over 200 compositions drawn from the uncertainty set, at the two fidelity levels of the process model. At the reactor level, the probability of meeting the 80% obligation is 38%; passing the same compositions through the full validated flowsheet — where flash and stabilizer losses and finite-conversion material count against the recovered liquid — lowers it to 24%. Each added layer of process fidelity makes compliance harder, not easier, so the reactor-level number is an upper bound on what any liquid-only accounting can deliver.

At the network level this risk proves *irreducible by siting*. In the base two-stage program, product revenues (₱36,200 t⁻¹ at central prices) dominate all cost terms, so every optimal design processes the entire contracted footprint; recovery then equals the mass-weighted mean yield of the stream, a quantity no arrangement of plants can change. Any all-processing configuration achieves 30% compliance probability over the fifty-scenario set — identical for the stochastic and the deterministic design, because the constraint binds on chemistry, not logistics. The single decision that moves this number is not spatial at all: one gas-energy-recovery module at the central site (₱15 M yr⁻¹ annualized), which credits fuel-gas energy recovery toward the obligation at η = 0.9, raises compliance from 30% to 80%. In the replication study of Section 3.3 this module is selected at the central site in 10/10 mild-heterogeneity and 8/10 strong-heterogeneity replications — the single structurally robust design feature of the study.

## 3.2 The value of the stochastic solution is zero — and why that is the finding

Across the base case and the full four-lever model, at both heterogeneity regimes, the value of the stochastic solution (VSS) is zero to within 0.012% of the objective at a 10⁻⁴ relative MIP gap. (At the coarser 5×10⁻³ gap the same experiments return small *negative* VSS values — theoretically impossible, and a useful warning that VSS accounting at loose solver tolerances manufactures artifacts.) Two mechanisms explain the genuine zero. First, the statutory penalty scale — ₱20 M lump fines, or twice the recovery cost of the shortfall — is two orders of magnitude below network operating profit (≈ ₱2.7 B yr⁻¹ at upper-bound economics), so a risk-neutral expectation barely registers non-compliance. Second, the one hedge that matters (the gas module) is already optimal at the *mean* composition, because mean liquid recovery (77.0%) itself falls short of the obligation; Jensen-type under-hedging never gets the chance to operate. The methodological conclusion generalizes: for profit-dominant valorization networks facing compliance obligations whose fines are small relative to margins, VSS systematically understates the value of stochastic modelling, because that value resides in the *risk constraint*, not the expected cost. The chance-constrained formulation of Section 3.3 is the appropriate lens, and the remainder of the results are organized around it.

## 3.3 The cost of compliance assurance

Table 3 and Fig. 5 report the study's central design quantity: the minimum additional annual cost of guaranteeing P(recovery ≥ 80%) ≥ 90%, relative to the risk-neutral optimum, from ten independent sample-average-approximation replications of one hundred scenarios per heterogeneity regime.

Under mild spatial heterogeneity ($c_{\mathrm{LGU}} = 60$) assurance is cheap and mechanistically uniform: ₱18.3 ± 9.9 M yr⁻¹ (mean ± 95% CI; median ₱10.0 M; range 3.6–41.5), achieved in every replication by hardware alone — a second gas module and, where needed, one capacity upsize. No replication drops a single LGU. Under strong heterogeneity ($c_{\mathrm{LGU}} = 15$) the mean rises to ₱849 ± 635 M yr⁻¹, but the mean is the wrong summary: the distribution is trimodal because the *assurance mechanism itself switches*. Four of ten replications remain in the hardware regime (₱6.5–22.5 M); four move to selective contracting, declining to serve one high-risk catchment (₱209–1,414 M, dominated by foregone revenue); and two find that the only way to guarantee compliance is to contract no footprint at all — the exit solutions discussed in Section 3.5. The median, ₱413 M yr⁻¹, is the honest central figure, roughly twenty-fold the mild-regime cost. Which LGU is dropped, when one is, is pure sampling noise: across replications the dropped catchment is Quezon City four times, Caloocan and Manila three times each, and others twice — no catchment is structurally condemned. What is structural is *that* selective contracting activates at all once spatial heterogeneity is strong, and since no Philippine dataset currently constrains within-metro resin heterogeneity, the twenty-fold spread between the regimes is itself the study's sharpest argument for commissioning resin-resolved waste characterization.

## 3.4 Deterministic designs over-promise compliance by 25–30 percentage points

The deterministic benchmark — the same model solved at the mean composition, then evaluated under the full scenario set — believes itself fully compliant: at the mean, with its gas module, the shortfall is zero and the design "promises" 100% compliance. Realized compliance under uncertainty is 75% (mild) and 70% (strong): an over-promise of 25–30 pp, or one violation year in three to four. Composition-blind fixed-yield formulations of the kind standard in the deterministic waste-network literature cannot even represent this risk, since a single yield coefficient admits no distribution at all. This quantifies, for the regulatory constraint that motivates the study, what is lost by the deterministic assumption — independently of whether any money is lost in expectation (Section 3.2 shows essentially none is).

## 3.5 An artifact found by replication: vacuous compliance by exit

Two strong-regime replications satisfied the chance constraint by contracting zero footprint: with no obligation denominator, 0 ≥ 0.8 × 0 holds vacuously, and "guaranteed compliance" is achieved by declining to participate. The formulation is faithful to the statute's letter — producer-responsibility organizations do contract voluntarily — but the solution is economically self-annihilating (it forfeits the entire ≈ ₱2.6 B yr⁻¹ operating profit) and regulatorily absurd. We report it rather than suppress it for two reasons: it was discovered by the replication protocol doing exactly what validation protocols are for, and it identifies a concrete refinement — a minimum-service constraint, or treating no-build as a separate branch of the analysis — that any statute-faithful compliance formulation of this class will require.

## 3.6 Design and economic synthesis

Every solve in the study, under every regime and formulation, selects the same spatial skeleton: one small northern satellite (15–30 kt yr⁻¹) plus one large central plant (120 kt yr⁻¹) — never full centralization, never full decentralization. Scale economies (capacity exponent 0.65) dominate transport (≈ ₱240 t⁻¹ typical), but the satellite persists for the northern catchments. The economics, propagated over the full uncertainty set (10⁴ Monte Carlo draws over composition, prices, and ±30% capital/operating cost), are strong but are reported in upper-bound form: break-even blended fuel price ₱15.1 L⁻¹ [P5–P95: 11.7–18.7] against a verified ex-plant diesel parity of ₱51–55 L⁻¹, which is better stated as a *cost headroom* — the design absorbs ₱26–30 k per tonne of costs not modelled (feedstock acquisition, collection, sorting, oil upgrading, land, working capital) before the net present value reaches zero, roughly twice literature-class estimates for those items combined. Global sensitivity analysis (Fig. 6) closes the argument: feedstock oil yield — that is, composition — carries a total Sobol index of 0.62 on NPV, exceeding all product prices and the ±30% capital range combined. The same uncertainty that breaks the compliance guarantee is also the dominant economic driver, and both point at the same missing dataset.


# 4. Discussion

**Relation to prior work.** Wang and Maravelias (2024) showed that composition-awareness changes plastic supply-chain design under a deterministic model; our results show what the deterministic assumption conceals — a 25–30 pp over-promise on the constraint that motivates the network's existence — while confirming their structural point, since composition dominates our economics as well (Sobol total index 0.62 on NPV). Relative to the deterministic siting lineage (Santibañez-Aguilar et al., 2013; Crîstiu et al., 2024), the spatial answer itself is stable: a hybrid configuration, never pure centralization or decentralization, robust across every regime and formulation we solved. What uncertainty changes is not where to build but what to guarantee, and at what price.

**When VSS misleads.** The near-zero value of the stochastic solution is, we argue, a property of a recognizable problem class rather than of this instance: valorization networks whose product margins dwarf their compliance penalties, and whose dominant hedge is already visible at the mean. In that class the standard two-stage expected-cost comparison will report that uncertainty does not matter while a quarter of scenarios violate the binding regulation. The cost-of-assurance curve — the price of a chance-constrained guarantee as a function of the guarantee level and of the uncertainty's structure — is the quantity a decision-maker actually needs, and it is invisible to VSS accounting. We also note a numerical hazard with practical teeth: at a conventional 0.5% MIP gap, VSS estimates in this study were spuriously negative; the accounting is only meaningful at tolerances an order of magnitude tighter than the quantity being measured.

**Policy reading.** Three implications follow directly from the numbers. First, under liquid-only recovery accounting the 80% obligation is close to infeasible for realistic Philippine residual streams regardless of network design — compliance is chemistry-bound — so the regulatory treatment of fuel-gas energy recovery (creditable or not under the implementing rules' diversion definitions) is the single most consequential accounting decision in the system; one ₱15 M yr⁻¹ module moves compliance from 30% to 80%. Second, statutory fines at their current scale do not economically force compliance for networks of this profitability; the binding form is the twice-recovery-cost provision, and enforcement design should recognize which arm of the penalty binds. Third, the twenty-fold spread in assurance cost between heterogeneity regimes — a parameter no existing dataset constrains — makes resin-resolved, locality-level waste characterization the cheapest risk-reduction instrument available: both the compliance analysis and the economic sensitivity point at the same measurement.

**Limitations.** The composition uncertainty set is constructed from verified qualitative structure and flagged magnitudes, not estimated from resin-resolved data — that is the premise, and the robust-set export allows the entire analysis to be re-posed distribution-free if challenged. Economics are deliberately upper-bound (no feedstock acquisition, collection, sorting, upgrading, land, or working capital) and are reported in cost-headroom form; if realized costs consume that ₱26–30 k t⁻¹ headroom, penalties become material and the VSS conclusion would be revisited. The yield basis is single-temperature (500 °C), matching the underlying experimental characterization; a temperature-resolved yield surface is a natural extension. The oracle is validated at six rigorous-simulation points spanning the set, not across it densely. The spatial instance is a prototype (eight catchments, four sites) with population-derived generation; and the chance-constrained formulation requires a minimum-service refinement to exclude the vacuous exit solution that our own replication protocol exposed.

# 5. Conclusions

For a Metro Manila mixed-plastic pyrolysis network under the RA 11898 recovery obligation: (i) compliance risk is chemistry-bound — a network processing its full contracted footprint meets the 80% obligation in 24–30% of composition scenarios, and no siting decision changes this, while one gas-energy-recovery module (₱15 M yr⁻¹) raises compliance to 80%; (ii) the value of the stochastic solution is ≈ 0 for identifiable reasons, and the decision-relevant quantity is the cost of compliance assurance — ₱18 M yr⁻¹ under mild spatial heterogeneity versus a median ₱413 M yr⁻¹, with a mechanism switch from hardware hedging to selective contracting, under strong heterogeneity; (iii) deterministic designs over-promise compliance by 25–30 percentage points; and (iv) feedstock composition carries 62% of NPV variance, exceeding all prices and capital uncertainty combined. The spatial answer is robustly hybrid. The single highest-value action outside the model is a measurement: resin-resolved, locality-level waste characterization, for which this study supplies two independent quantitative justifications.
